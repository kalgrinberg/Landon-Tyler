The History of the 1970 AMC Javelin AMX

American Motors Corporation was the smallest of the Big Four automakers, but it refused to
sit out the muscle car wars. The Javelin, introduced in 1968, was AMC's entry into the
pony car segment, and the AMX variant represented the company's most serious performance
statement.

The original 1968-1969 AMX was a two-seat sports car, unique among American muscle cars.
For 1970, AMC redesigned the Javelin and folded the AMX name into the lineup as the top
performance package rather than a separate model. The 1970 Javelin received dramatic new
styling with a distinctive "twin venturi" shape, a prominent cowl induction hood, and
aggressive wheel arches.

The 1970 Javelin AMX came standard with a 360 cubic-inch V8, but the real performer was
the optional 390 cubic-inch engine producing 325 horsepower and 420 lb-ft of torque.
This engine featured a forged steel crankshaft, high-flow cylinder heads, and a
four-barrel carburetor. A "Go Package" added a Ram Air induction system, dual exhaust,
handling package, and E70x14 Polyglas tires.

AMC's secret weapon in the performance wars was Mark Donohue and Roger Penske's racing
team. The Javelin competed in the SCCA Trans-Am series from 1968 through 1972, winning
the championship in 1971 and 1972. Donohue's success on the track lent credibility to the
Javelin as a legitimate performance car, not just a budget alternative.

A special Mark Donohue signature edition was offered for 1970 and 1971, featuring a rear
spoiler designed by Donohue himself, unique striping, and special identification. The 1971
version also included a functional ducted hood that directed air to the engine.

The 1970 Javelin AMX could run the quarter-mile in the mid-14-second range in stock form,
competitive with many of its Big Three rivals. Total AMX production for 1970 was
approximately 2,501 units.

While often overlooked compared to Mopar and GM muscle cars, the AMC Javelin AMX has
gained significant collector appreciation in recent decades. Its racing heritage, unique
styling, and relative rarity make it an increasingly valuable piece of muscle car history.
