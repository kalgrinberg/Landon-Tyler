The History of the 1970 Plymouth Hemi 'Cuda

The 1970 Plymouth Hemi 'Cuda is widely regarded as the holy grail of muscle cars. Built on
the redesigned E-body platform, the 1970 Barracuda was a dramatic departure from the earlier
A-body Barracudas that had been produced since 1964. Plymouth shortened the name to 'Cuda
for the performance versions, distinguishing them from the base Barracuda models.

The crown jewel of the 'Cuda lineup was the 426 Hemi engine, a 426 cubic-inch (7.0-liter)
V8 that produced a conservatively rated 425 horsepower and 490 lb-ft of torque. The
"elephant motor," as it was nicknamed, featured hemispherical combustion chambers that
allowed for superior airflow and more efficient combustion than conventional wedge-head
designs. Dual four-barrel Carter AFB carburetors sat atop the engine.

The 1970 'Cuda featured aggressive styling with a shaker hood scoop that protruded through
the hood and vibrated with the engine, billboard side stripes, and an optional rear wing.
Plymouth offered the car in a dazzling array of "High Impact" colors including Lime Light,
Lemon Twist, In-Violet, Tor-Red, and the famous Plum Crazy purple.

The Hemi 'Cuda was brutally fast, capable of 0-60 mph in approximately 5.6 seconds and
quarter-mile times in the mid-13-second range. However, the Hemi option added roughly
$900 to the base price, which was a significant amount in 1970, and the engine was
notoriously finicky for street use.

Only 652 Hemi 'Cudas were produced for the 1970 model year in hardtop form, with a mere
14 convertibles built. The convertible Hemi 'Cuda is among the rarest and most valuable
American cars in existence, with examples selling for over $3 million at auction.

Production continued through 1971 with even fewer Hemi 'Cudas built (108 total), before
the engine option was discontinued. The combination of rarity, stunning looks, and raw
Hemi power has made the 1970 Hemi 'Cuda the single most collectible muscle car ever built.
