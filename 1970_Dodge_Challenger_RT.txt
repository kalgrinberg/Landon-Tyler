The History of the 1970 Dodge Challenger R/T

The 1970 Dodge Challenger R/T arrived as Dodge's answer to the pony car wars that had been
raging since the Ford Mustang debuted in 1964. Sharing the E-body platform with the
Plymouth Barracuda, the Challenger was longer, wider, and more luxurious than its Plymouth
sibling, targeting buyers who wanted muscle with a touch of refinement.

The "R/T" designation stood for "Road/Track," and it came standard with the 383 cubic-inch
Magnum V8 producing 335 horsepower. However, the real excitement lay in the optional
engines: the 440 Six Pack with three two-barrel carburetors producing 390 horsepower, and
the legendary 426 Hemi rated at 425 horsepower.

Dodge also created the Challenger T/A (Trans-Am) variant for 1970, designed to homologate
the car for SCCA Trans-Am racing. The T/A featured a 340 cubic-inch Six Pack engine, a
matte-black fiberglass hood with a massive scoop, side-exit exhaust pipes, and a rear
spoiler. Only 2,399 T/A models were built.

The Challenger R/T was offered in both hardtop and convertible body styles. The SE (Special
Edition) package added a vinyl roof, leather seats, and additional luxury appointments.
Like the 'Cuda, the Challenger was available in Dodge's vivid "High Impact" colors including
Plum Crazy, Go Mango, Sub Lime, Hemi Orange, and Top Banana.

On the track, the Hemi Challenger could cover the quarter-mile in approximately 13.5 seconds.
The car featured heavy-duty suspension, front disc brakes, and Rallye wheels as standard
R/T equipment.

The Challenger continued through the 1974 model year, but the muscle had largely been
drained by 1972 due to emissions requirements and insurance industry pressure. First-year
1970 models remain the most desirable, particularly Hemi-powered cars and convertibles.
The Challenger name was revived by Dodge in 2008, paying direct homage to the classic
1970 design.
