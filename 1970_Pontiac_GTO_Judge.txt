The History of the 1970 Pontiac GTO Judge

The Pontiac GTO is widely credited as the car that started the muscle car era. When Pontiac
engineer John DeLorean and his team dropped a 389 cubic-inch V8 into the mid-size Tempest
in 1964, they created a formula that every other manufacturer would rush to copy. The name
"GTO" stood for "Gran Turismo Omologato," boldly borrowed from the Ferrari 250 GTO.

By 1969, the muscle car market was crowded, and Pontiac introduced "The Judge" option
package to add visual excitement and maintain the GTO's relevance. Named after a recurring
comedy sketch on the television show "Laugh-In" ("Here come da Judge!"), the package added
bold striping, a rear wing spoiler, Rallye II wheels, and "The Judge" decals.

The 1970 GTO Judge received the updated second-generation A-body styling with a more
rounded, aggressive look. The standard engine was the Ram Air III 400 cubic-inch V8
producing 366 horsepower, but the ultimate option was the Ram Air IV, a radical engine
with round-port heads, a wilder camshaft, and aluminum intake manifold that produced
370 horsepower (again, widely believed to be underrated).

The 1970 Judge also offered the massive 455 cubic-inch V8 as an option, producing 360
horsepower with a more streetable torque curve. The Ram Air IV remained the choice for
serious performance enthusiasts, however, capable of propelling the GTO through the
quarter-mile in the high 13-second range.

The Judge package was available in Orbit Orange as the standard color for 1969, but by
1970 it could be ordered in any GTO color. A total of 3,797 Judge hardtops and just 168
Judge convertibles were produced for 1970.

The GTO continued through 1974, but the Judge option was dropped after 1971. The 1970
GTO Judge represents the perfect combination of Pontiac's muscle car heritage and late-era
flamboyance, making it highly prized by collectors who appreciate both its performance
credentials and its place in automotive history.
