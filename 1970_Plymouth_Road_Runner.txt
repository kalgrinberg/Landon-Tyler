The History of the 1970 Plymouth Road Runner

The Plymouth Road Runner was conceived as the everyman's muscle car. When it debuted in
1968, it was designed to strip away luxury and focus purely on performance at an affordable
price. Plymouth licensed the Road Runner name and character from Warner Bros., and the car
even featured a horn that mimicked the cartoon bird's famous "beep beep" sound.

The original 1968 Road Runner came standard with a 383 cubic-inch V8 producing 335
horsepower, a four-speed manual transmission, heavy-duty suspension, and minimal creature
comforts. It was a deliberate throwback to the hot rod ethos of putting a big engine in a
light, no-frills car. The formula was an instant hit, with Plymouth selling over 44,000
units in its first year, far exceeding the projected 2,500.

By 1970, the Road Runner received the updated B-body styling shared with the Satellite.
The design was more rounded and aggressive than the previous generation, with a distinctive
loop front bumper and available Air Grabber hood that raised a scoop at the push of a
dashboard-mounted button, revealing a cartoon Road Runner graphic underneath.

Engine options for 1970 included the standard 383, the optional 440 cubic-inch Six Barrel
(with three two-barrel Holley carburetors) producing 390 horsepower, and the ultimate
426 Hemi rated at 425 horsepower. The 440+6 was the sweet spot for many buyers, offering
near-Hemi performance at a fraction of the cost.

The 1970 Road Runner also spawned the legendary Plymouth Superbird, the aero-warrior
version with a towering rear wing and extended nose cone designed for NASCAR dominance.
Only 1,920 Superbirds were produced, and they have become some of the most recognizable
and valuable American cars ever built.

Total 1970 Road Runner production was approximately 41,484 units, making it one of the
higher-volume muscle cars. The Road Runner continued through 1975, but post-1970 models
suffered from declining power and increasing weight. The 1970 model year represents the
last hurrah for the full-strength Road Runner, combining affordability, simplicity, and
brutal straight-line speed.
