The History of the 1970 Buick GSX

The 1970 Buick GSX is one of the most underappreciated muscle cars of the era, yet it was
arguably one of the most potent. Buick had long been associated with luxury and refinement
rather than raw performance, but the GSX shattered that image completely.

The GSX (Gran Sport Experimental) was introduced in 1970 as the ultimate version of the
Buick Skylark Gran Sport. It was based on GM's A-body platform and came standard with
Buick's massive 455 cubic-inch (7.5-liter) V8 engine. In Stage 1 tune, this engine
produced a factory-rated 360 horsepower and an earth-moving 510 lb-ft of torque, the
highest torque figure of any muscle car engine of the era.

The Stage 1 package included a revised camshaft, larger valves, special cylinder heads,
and a recalibrated Quadrajet four-barrel carburetor. Like most manufacturers, Buick
significantly underrated the engine's output. Independent tests suggested the true
horsepower figure was closer to 400 or more.

Visually, the GSX was impossible to miss. It came in only two colors for 1970: Apollo
White or Saturn Yellow, both with bold black stripes, a rear spoiler, hood-mounted
tachometer, front and rear spoilers, Rallye wheels, and GSX badging. The overall effect
was dramatic and aggressive, a stark contrast to typical Buick styling.

Performance was devastating. The Stage 1 GSX could reach 60 mph in approximately 5.5
seconds and run the quarter-mile in the low to mid 13-second range. Its immense torque
made it particularly effective from a roll, and many racers found the Buick could pull
away from supposedly faster competitors in real-world driving.

Only 678 GSX models were produced for 1970, with 488 equipped with the Stage 1 engine.
The GSX continued for 1971 and 1972, but in reduced form with lower compression ratios
and declining sales. The 1970 Stage 1 GSX remains one of the most collectible muscle
cars, prized for its combination of luxury, brutish performance, and extreme rarity.
